VidPT Redesign

case study

project info

duration

fall 2019-present

role

ui designer

ux designer

overview

VidPT is a physical therapy mobile application that allows patients to do their rehab routine on their own or with supervision from a physical therapist(PT).

problem statement

During September 2018-March 2019, my senior design team were tasked with helping VidPT create a mobile app. The app sought to relieve physical therapy pain points such as patients starting their rehab routine late due to medical insurance, trying to understand drawings and instructions for exercises, and help patients feel motivated through their journey. However, the team created an MVP that didn’t have all the features implemented. Now, VidPT is looking for a redesign of the app to attract more users in need of physical therapy and physical therapists.

Figure 1. Original design of the VidPT homescreen

evaluation of current app

I talked with the founder, Michael about the features he wanted to implement and evaluated the state of the app. The app has the following problems:
• Limited progress information
• No personalization for solo patients
• No physical therapist interface to connect with patients
• Fails to keep users engaged
• Looks like a regular workout app

goals

1. Make physical therapy easier to understand
2. Motivate users through progress tracking
3. Create a positive community amongst patients
4. Connect PT's and patients

competitive analysis

Figure 2. Competitive analysis

Before getting started, I decided to compare existing fitness applications to view their features. The team decided that we should allow users to do a rehab routine on their own. Most routines are standard and the routine our users would receive would be created with input from a physical therapist. I decided it would be best to ignore the timer feature. Physical therapy focuses on performing each exercise correctly and we want to prevent injuries.

user research

surveys and interviews

An online survey was conducted asking former patients about their injury, attending physical therapy, and doing their rehab routine on their own. 30-minute interviews were conducted with former physical therapy patients and a physical therapist.

findings

I analyzed over 200 survey results and 4 interview transcripts and found the following information.

■ Target audience is between the ages 31-45.

■ Top issues with the physical therapy process are cost/insurance, communication with the physical therapist, and time spent in the PT office.

■ 70% of patients receive sketch drawings or handwritten notes about their routine.

■ Rehab routine and intensity is based on the rehab goal of the patient.

Testing

usability test

Low-fidelity prototypes were tested amongst potential users and a physical therapist. Usability tests focused on signing up, completing a routine, and viewing patients. Examples of how the scenarios were structured can be found below.

Figure 4. Wireframe of homescreen

Task 1: Imagine you are a patient diagnosed with IT Band Syndrome and decided to go on your own recovery journey. Sign up for a solo account on the app.

Task 2: Imagine you are a patient eager to start their workout but want to make some changes. How would you go about it?

Task 3: Imagine you had a meeting with your patient and they are interested in using the mobile app. How would you go about adding the patient on the app?

Cognitive Walkthrough

There was a moment when I thought the design was good enough but that was because I had been working on the flow for so many hours. I conducted a cognitive walkthrough with the same cases from the usability test and the main goal of the walkthrough was to see if the users could recognize the next step in the flow.

Results

The signup process for patients was too long (8-9 screens) and there was no indicator stating where in the process the user was at. My solution is to add an indicator or progress bar and reduce the number of interactions and screens in the process. When testing with the PT, they mentioned that the home page lacked content. When I asked what type of information is prioritized in their day to day work, they mentioned knowing which patients needed to be seen immediately, such as those who were not making progress.

focus area

- What information do patients and physical therapists want to know?

- How do we encourage people throughout their recovery process?

- How can the rehab routine become understandable and easier to perform?

- Best way to connect physical therapists and patients?

solutions

use of video

Unlike a diagram or an image, looking at a video allows the user to see how the exercise is performed from different angles. The user doesn’t have to try recalling how the therapist performed the move since the video will always be there for reference. VidPTs exercise videos have audio which will provide more information on how to properly perform each move.

track progress through weekly surveys

After each week, patients will be prompted to take a progress survey that asks about their current pain level, current strength level, and ability to do everyday things. According to our interview with the physical therapist, this is vital information to determine a new routine for patients.

There is also a Workout Average Chart that allows users to see how much time they spend doing their rehab routine and the Calendar Tracker allows patients to view days they rested, skipped, and worked out.

Figure 5. Weekly survey and progress tab

Figure 6. Brag Wall tab

brag wall

A common issue found in our interview and survey data is that physical therapy was feeling lonely due to not having people with a similar experience around. The Brag Wall is the community section of the app which allows patients to connect with one another by posting questions, accomplishments, and positive messages.

solo routine

Patients using the app without the PT assistance can edit their routine with a library of color-coded exercises that target their specific injury. For each exercise there is a recommended amount of sets/reps which can only be adjusted to a smaller amount. I suggested adding new exercises in the library for a patient if they made progress on the weekly surveys to mimic how in-person physical therapy.

Patients can add their own notes to view during their routine. However, if the patient is working with a physical therapist, then the PT is responsible for making the changes to the routine such as picking exercises and adding notes.

physical therapists connect

Physical Therapists can monitor their patients progress through the app. They are able to add a patient, edit a patient’s routine, and view their progress. PTs are notified when a patient completes a weekly progress check or not performing well and can view/use those results to make changes to the routine. This information is reflected on the home screen. At the moment, PTs cannot message a patient through the app but this feature will be designed for future iterations.

reflections

As of right now, the project is ongoing and there will be more testing and design changes.

Through this experience, I am learning how to improve my research and testing skills. I gathered great information from former patients but I need to learn more about the physical therapist role. I believe an ethnographic study on physical therapists would help reveal their routine and I can learn more about what needs to be improved between the PT-Patient relation. Also, I am learning how to improve the interview protocol to gather relevant information. I am analyzing each question and asking myself what is the purpose of the question and what do I want to get out of it? I will be implementing these new research techniques while the app is still ongoing.
