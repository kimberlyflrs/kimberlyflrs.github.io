VidPT: A Physical Therapy App

The VidPT mobile app allows users to create their own personalized rehab routine or with supervision from a physical therapist. Users can track their progress, interact with users, and add notes to each exercise.

Duration: January 2020 - August 2020

Problem

  • Users found it difficult to continue their rehab routine with loss of insurance.
  • Users found it difficult to perform their routine according to the notes provided.
  • Competitor apps do not consider personalized routines based on a patient’s progress.

Goal

  • Design a rehab routine flow.
  • Consider user scenarios and needs.
  • Increase user retention rate.

Action

  • Users can understand their routine through visuals and written notes.
  • Add progress tracking metrics.
  • Support user interactions through posts and comments.

Result

  • The app is currently in production.

Our Goal

We aim to create a product that can improve the physical therapy experience and allow users to receive the help they need. Since this is a new app in the making, it was important to build the user base and find our target audience.

My Role

I was responsible for the product design and conducting user research. I created quick prototype iterations and optimized the design flow according to user feedback. I worked closely with the marketing team, development team, and the project manager.

Design Process

1. User Research

An online survey was conducted asking former patients about their injury, attending physical therapy sessions, and doing their rehab routine on their own. I conducted 30 minute interviews with former patients and a physical therapist to understand the therapy experience. I analyzed over 200 survey results and 4 interview transcripts and found the following information.

Target user analysis based on survey and interviews

  • 70% of patients receive sketch drawings or handwritten notes about their routine. Users often have to look up external sources to understand their routine.
  • Users do not know their progress until they go visit their physical therapist.
  • Cost/Insurance, Lack of PT communication, and time spent with PT are the reasons users drop out of their physical therapy.
  • Personalization.Users receive basic routines from their PT but there is personalization according to each patient’s progress.
  • Users consider the recovery journey a lonesome one. There are not a lot of support groups or they cannot find someone to talk to going through the same experience.

3. Design Considerations

  • Add multiple ways to understand routine. Traditional PT patients receive diagrams or paper notes describing each exercise. However, they still look up the exercise online to know how to do each move and jot down more notes for understanding.
  • Add progress metrics. 65% past patients mentioned that it is difficult to know if they were making any progress.
  • Personalize each routine. In order to mimic how PTs evaluate each patient, they told us that they start with a generic routine and based on the progress, they start changing the sets/reps, adding/removing exercises based on the patient’s ability.
  • Connect with others. Former patients wish they could talk to someone that has gone through the same experience.
  • Connect with PT. For the users that are working with a PT, it is important to have some way to receive updates from them

4. Design Iterations

Low Fidelity Wireframes and Prototypes

I invited stakeholders to check out a clickable wireframe set made on Adobe XD. We went through each screen but it was proving to be inefficient. I then composed a visual that encompassed all the screens. This allowed us to view the content more clearly and see how each screen is connected to one another. It also helped with identifying any problems. We took notes about individual screens and the overall user flow.

5. Mid Fidelity Wireframes

Progress Tracking

We asked former patients what metrics they would have liked to have known throughout their journey and agreed to track the following:

  1. Calendar Tracker [Keep track of routines completed]
  2. Strength/Pain levels
  3. Routines Completed
  4. Time Sent

Editing Routines

When talking to former patients, they mentioned how they would look up videos of each exercise in order to perform it correctly and write out their own notes. The components added to the Edit Screens are:

  1. Video with audio describing the movement
  2. Adjust sets and reps
  3. Descriptive notes

Interacting with other users

We want to create a supportive community by allowing patients to connect with one another and sharing their experiences through posts. Users can make a comment and like each post. Users can check out other user’s profiles and learn more about them.

Interacting with Physical Therapists

It was important to have a way for patients to still receive care from their PT so we developed a Web Application for PTs to keep track of their patients, send notes, and modify the patient’s routine. Users will receive messages through notifications and see PT notes on a designated tab. Web App Case Study Coming Soon.

6. Usability Testing

I created low-fidelity prototypes to test 5 users who did and didn’t have physical therapy experience. The test focused on the sign up process, editing and completing a routine, progress checks, and interacting with other users. We looked at how users interact with the app, what are the users’ needs, and in what context do they use each feature.

Feedback from participants

  • Participants found the sign up process too long. Initially the sign up process contained a custom quiz to get a personalize routine but the questions were too much.
  • Most participants found it difficult to add reminders to each exercise. The reminders were hidden behind a ‘+’ dropdown and users often skipped this area.
  • Most participants agreed that a weekly check on their progress was a good decision. Anything more frequent would be considered tiresome and anything less would prove the progress section useless.
  • Participants had a difficult time finding the make a post button located on the upper-left hand corner.

7. Iteration Based on Research Findings

Sign Up Flow

After talking to the marketing team, we decided to move the custom starter routine quiz to be part of the premium plan. I minimized the number of screens needed to sign up from 8 to 3 screens.

Edit Routine Flow

I removed the reminders from being tucked in the ‘+’ icon to having them available in a different section where the option of making a note is always available.

Posting

I moved the Post icon to the lower right hand corner as a floating button. The icon was also changed from a ‘+’ sign to a pencil and paper icon.

What I learned

After finishing the final version, I presented the prototype to my boss and development team but they had a difficult time understanding the flow. In order to address this problem, I decided to break up the prototype into different use cases. I also created a document that highlights how each screen is connected and the interactions for each one.

Conclusion

I wish I could’ve conducted more usability tests in person but we had to halt it due to Covid-19. However, I was able to adjust to the situation and I conducted the tests remotely using Useberry and had calls with our participants about the test. I made a clear plan to explain to the test users what tasks they were going to accomplish on their computer screens. The alternative plan allowed us to stay on track with the project and save resources.
